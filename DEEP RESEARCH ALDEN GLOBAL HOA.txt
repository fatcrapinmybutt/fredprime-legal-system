## Patterns Indicative of RICO & Fraudulent Scheme

**Interstate Entity Abuse Pattern:** The findings above describe a classic **pattern of racketeering activity** spanning multiple states over multiple years:

* **Multiple Victims (Geographically Dispersed):** Residents in Michigan, Florida, Illinois, and beyond have suffered from the same practices by Homes of America/Alden. Lot rents were hiked exorbitantly (often 20–98% increases) and fees tacked on; those who couldn’t pay faced aggressive evictions (Alden’s Florida parks had an eviction rate far above average at 68% of filings leading to vacate orders). Meanwhile, promised services (water, maintenance) were withheld. This **“profits over people”** scheme has been documented by advocacy reports and prosecutors as **intentional and coordinated**.

* **Use of Mail/Wires:** It is reasonable to infer that rent invoices, eviction notices, payment processing, and corporate communications all traveled via mail or electronic means. For instance, residents receive written notices of rent changes and pay rent electronically or by mail to a centralized office. In North Morris Estates’ case, the **Englewood, NJ office** was receiving official correspondence (and presumably handling billing). This satisfies the **interstate commerce** element: the enterprise’s activities cross state lines, and they utilize interstate communication to further the scheme (e.g., transmitting false reassurances to residents or concealing the true ownership in communications).

* **Predicate Offenses:** Potential predicate offenses under RICO (18 U.S.C. §1961) evident in this scenario include: **Mail and Wire Fraud** (18 U.S.C. §§1341, 1343) – as discussed, systematically charging for habitable housing while concealing or misrepresenting the deplorable, unlawful conditions can be a scheme to defraud. Also, **extortion** under color of economic harm could be argued (18 U.S.C. §1951 Hobbs Act) – Alden effectively extorts higher rents by threatening eviction, exploiting residents’ lack of mobility (though this is a novel application, some courts have considered landlord rent gouging + eviction threats as extortionate). The consistent thread is the **intentional deceptive and coercive behavior** to increase revenue. Michigan law also has a **racketeering statute** (Mich. Comp. Laws § 750.159g) which includes as racketeering acts any violation of certain fraud statutes and even *“obtaining services or property by false pretenses”*. Operating a park without a license while continuing to collect rent could be viewed as obtaining property (rent money) under false pretenses (implying legally operating and compliant status). The Michigan **Consumer Protection Act** might also be invoked for deceptive business practices (though there’s an exemption for regulated entities, one could argue an unlicensed park isn’t a “regulated business” and thus subject to the Act).

* **Enterprise and Conspiracy:** The enterprise here is **Alden Global Capital’s mobile home park network** – consisting of Alden itself, Homes of America LLC, and all the shell LLCs acting in concert. The evidence shows an **“association-in-fact” enterprise**: a group of entities and individuals unified by common ownership and purpose, working together in a continuing unit to achieve the scheme of maximizing profits from mobile home parks through unlawful means. We have named individuals (Smith, Khalil, etc.) and companies (LLCs) that are part of it. The **pattern** is ongoing (not a closed period – issues persist up to the present, e.g., 2024 legislative hearings referencing dirty water and abuses). Thus, both the *open-ended continuity* and *closed-ended continuity* requirements for RICO could be met. Each park’s saga (from acquisition, through neglect, to either enforcement or continuing harm) is like a chapter in a larger story of exploitation.

* **Intent and Knowledge:** It will be crucial to show Alden/Homes of America **knew** of the problems and **intentionally chose** profit over remedies. We already have strong indications: *“Homes of America’s mismanagement”* was extensively noted, and even after official notices (like EGLE’s letters or LARA’s warnings), the company failed to act. The Genesee prosecutor stated that this seemed to be **“part of the company’s corporate strategy across the United States”** to slash costs including vital services. That quote is telling – a law enforcement official observed a *strategic pattern*, which goes to intent. The legislative push in Michigan (SB 486-492, HB 5157-5163) was triggered by these events and explicitly tied to **Alden’s bad behavior (outrageous rent hikes, ‘dirty water’, etc.)**. This can be used to demonstrate that even lawmakers perceive the conduct as abusive and coordinated. If needed, statements by Alden’s executives in other contexts (for example, Alden’s well-known strategy of cutting costs at newspapers it buys) could be analogized – the **modus operandi** is consistent: acquire, extract value, neglect obligations. Such evidence is circumstantial but persuasive for a jury to understand the **motive** (greed) and **method** (cutting expenses like water treatment, maintenance, licensing fees, etc., and hiding behind corporate layers).

* **Financial Trail:** Alden’s end-goal was clearly financial. Notably, PESP’s report found that **Alden took out hundreds of millions in mortgages on these parks after purchase, leveraging them with debt**. This allowed them to pull cash out upfront. If those loans were obtained by overstating the condition or value of the parks (for instance, not disclosing that infrastructure was failing or that they were violating laws), that could be **bank fraud** or at least further evidence of a fraudulent pattern. It might be beyond the scope of the current inquiry, but it’s a lead: check mortgage documents and see if any misrepresentations were made to lenders (somewhat outside RICO, but relevant to show a pattern of dishonest dealings). Also, **trace rent revenue** – if Alden commingled funds or siphoned money from LLC accounts to the hedge fund without reinvestment, it bolsters alter ego claims. It’s already public that these parks generate significant NOI (net operating income); e.g., Shady Oaks had \~\$94k NOI in 2019 under prior ownership, and Alden’s rent hikes would increase that. Yet virtually none of that appears to have been reinvested in park improvements, which a jury can interpret as exploitation.

It’s essentially **parallel conduct with a common motive**, which when combined with the structural links, confirms it’s a centrally directed scheme.

  * **Illinois** (Metro-East St. Louis area): News reports indicate Homes of America bought parks there and exploited tenants, as noted by an advocacy report accusing Alden of “exploiting vulnerable tenants”.

  Every state’s example reinforces that this is not a one-off or localized issue – it’s an *organized business model* by Alden’s entities. This goes a long way to proving the “pattern” element for RICO (which requires continuity and relatedness). The relatedness is clear: all acts have similar purpose (increase profit), similar results (harm to tenants, regulatory breaches), similar participants (same companies), and similar victims (manufactured homeowners) – satisfying the **Morgan v. Bank of Waukegan** factors for RICO pattern relatedness.

* **Possible Federal Enforcement:** While our focus is civil litigation, note that the **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)** and **FTC** have shown interest in mobile home park issues recently. The CFPB in 2022 issued a report on manufactured housing community financing and risks. It’s possible to engage these agencies or the **Department of Justice Civil Division** (which can bring civil RICO or other actions for systemic housing discrimination or fraud). If any protected classes are disproportionately affected (e.g., many elderly or disabled residents were impacted by water issues – North Morris had many older residents as per Gantos’ statement), there could even be a **Fair Housing Act** angle (constructive eviction of disabled residents due to lack of habitability). These angles might augment a RICO case or provide parallel enforcement tracks.

* **Monitoring Future Conduct:** Since the litigation is ongoing, one recommendation is to seek a **preliminary injunction or court-appointed receiver** for these parks. The evidence of mismanagement is strong – a court could be persuaded to appoint a receiver to manage the properties so that further harm is mitigated (ensuring licenses are maintained, water is safe, etc.). This would not only protect residents (a worthy goal on its own) but also prevent Alden from **draining assets or collapsing entities** during the suit. RICO allows equitable relief in some cases, and under Michigan law, a receivership can be sought for entities operating unlawfully. Given that one sale (North Morris) will happen by court order, we might suggest extending such oversight to other parks preemptively.

In conclusion, the LARA and EGLE records combined paint a picture of an **interstate racketeering enterprise** in the mobile home park sector. Corporate filings show a web of LLCs **controlled by a single New York-based financial firm (Alden)**. Environmental and licensing records show those entities engaging in **repeated legal violations** – from operating without mandatory licenses to providing water that violates health standards – as part of a **profit-maximizing scheme**. The involvement of specific individuals connects the dots between corporate policy and on-the-ground actions, helping to assign knowledge and intent to the enterprise.

**Recommended Legal Actions:** We have grounds to integrate these findings into several legal strategies:

* **State Enforcement:** Encourage Michigan authorities (LARA, AG’s office) to pursue further actions on any outstanding violations (e.g., if any other park is near non-compliance, act now before it becomes another North Morris). The Mobile Home Commission Act provides administrative penalty pathways too – those could be leveraged to build the record.
* **Civil Litigation (Residents vs. Owners):** Use the evidence to support a **civil RICO lawsuit** on behalf of affected residents in federal court. Include claims for fraud, breach of contract (of lot leases, which implicitly or explicitly require providing basic services), and violations of state consumer protection laws. Seek class action certification if appropriate (residents of each park, or even a multi-state class given the common plan – though multi-state class has complexities).
* **Piercing the Veil:** Argue that the corporate veil of these LLCs should be pierced due to **undercapitalization and unity of interest**. The LARA records of common addresses/agents and the siphoning of funds (if shown) will support that **Homes of America LLC and Alden** are directly liable for the LLCs’ debts and liabilities.
* **Criminal Referral:** Provide this dossier to the Michigan Attorney General and U.S. Attorney. The Genesee County Prosecutor’s success could inspire broader criminal RICO or fraud charges. Particularly, federal prosecutors might look at mail/wire fraud across state lines (bringing in the heavy hammer of criminal RICO which has more severe penalties). Even the **threat of criminal charges** can bring a recalcitrant corporate defendant to the table for a global resolution (e.g., forcing Alden to divest all its Michigan parks under court supervision, as happened with North Morris, and possibly pay restitution to residents for harm caused).

Finally, this investigation underscores why regulatory gaps must be closed (as Michigan’s pending legislation aims to do). The **loophole in drinking water oversight** (whereby small private utilities like those in parks evaded some scrutiny) and **lack of pre-sale vetting of park buyers** allowed a hedge fund with a notorious reputation to cause havoc. From a litigation standpoint, highlighting these loopholes and abuses not only strengthens the case narrative but also builds public pressure.

**Conclusion:** The LARA and EGLE records provide compelling evidence of a **coordinated scheme of interstate corporate abuse** in the operation of manufactured home parks. By weaving these records together – corporate filings showing how the parks are held and managed, and regulatory reports showing what the managers did (or failed to do) – we can demonstrate the elements of fraud, pattern, and enterprise required under RICO, as well as multiple violations of Michigan law (Mobile Home Commission Act, Safe Drinking Water Act, etc.). The information strongly supports litigation to hold the responsible entities accountable and to prevent further harm to the thousands of residents living in these communities.
